The artisanal mining landscape in DRC

Bennett Hanson

Data is from the International Peace Information Service and filtered to only include the mines visited by investigators in Democratic Republic of Congo between October 2020 and March 2023.

An estimated 1 million miners toil as informal, artisanal miners in DRC, a country rich in resources but plagued by over 100 armed groups. Access to the artisanal mining sites — primarily gold, tin and coltan — is a source of conflict among the armed groups and often their main source of income. Because DRC's army (FARDC), police (PNC) and mining police (PMH) are often present at the mines (and involved in the corruption as well), IPIS includes these state services in the armed group category.

I analyzed the data from the mines visited by IPIS between October 2020 and March 2023 to look for correlations between the presence of armed groups, the use of mercury and the use of child labor. See my findings below.

People with allergies are particularly affected

More than 70 % of the defective products destined for Swiss supermarkets and restaurants have been categorised by the authorities as serious. If there is plastic in Swiss milk chocolate or E. coli bacteria in French mussels, the consequences can be devastating. Even undeclared ingredients - such as nuts in cookies from Austria - pose a danger to people with allergies.

As soon as a European authority discovers problematic products, it triggers an international alert. This must happen as quickly as possible, even if the danger level is still unclear. That is why some of the recalled goods are not categorised. A large proportion of these products nevertheless end up on the market before they are withdrawn from circulation.

Among the food items for the Swiss market that pose a serious threat, spices were the most frequently affected. Items such as bakery products or food additives were also often deficient. This is in absolute figures. Spices are affected by recalls disproportionately to the sales volume.

Fake oregano is common

Spices are also often affected by counterfeiting. This was shown by an analysis of 23 European countries between 2019 and 2021. Nearly 10,000 analyses were carried out. The percentage of samples which were deemed at risk of adulteration were 17% for pepper, 14% for cumin, 11% for curcuma, 11% for saffron and 6% for paprika/chilli. Oregano was identified as the most vulnerable with 48% of samples at risk of contamination, with olive leaves in most cases.

Among the 649 products recalled for the Swiss market with dangerous defects were also over 50 organic articles. With a share of 8%, however, they are thus affected with below-average frequency. Organic articles account for a total of 11% of the market in Switzerland, according to the association Bio-Suisse. Consumers who buy organic goods can therefore feel somewhat safer. However, the purity of products is by no means guaranteed even for organic, as the figures from the European authorities show.
